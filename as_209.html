Location

AS 209 is located in the Ophiuchus molecular cloud, a nearby star-forming region that hosts many young stellar systems. In the literature, the source is described as a T Tauri star in the young Ophiuchus star-forming region at a distance of 126 pc from the Sun (Fedele et al. 2018).

Its relative proximity makes AS 209 an excellent target for high-resolution millimeter observations, allowing both the dust and gas substructures of the disk to be studied in detail.

Overview of the system

“The formation of planets occurs within protoplanetary disks surrounding young stars, resulting in perturbation of the gas and dust surface densities.”

AS 209 is a protoplanetary disk with a remarkably structured radial morphology. Early ALMA continuum observations showed that the dust emission is organized into a central component plus two prominent rings, separated by two clear dark gaps (Fedele et al. 2018). In particular, the 1.3 mm continuum profile was described as having rings at 75 AU and 130 AU, with gaps at 62 AU and 103 AU (Fedele et al. 2018).

Later high-resolution observations revealed that the disk is even more complex, with additional substructures at smaller and intermediate radii. In the DSHARP analysis, AS 209 was highlighted as a system with many gaps, reinforcing its importance as a benchmark object for studying disk substructures and their physical origins (Zhang et al. 2018).

Molecular-line observations also show that the gas disk is structured. In particular, the optically thin C\(^{18}\)O emission displays a compact central component within ~60 AU and an outer gas ring peaking near 140 AU, while thermo-chemical modeling indicates that the gas surface density is reduced in radial intervals associated with the dust gaps (Favre et al. 2019). Taken together, these observations make AS 209 one of the clearest examples of a disk in which both dust and gas are dynamically perturbed.

Disk morphology

ALMA observations show that the disk of AS 209 contains a structured radial pattern of bright rings and dark gaps in the millimeter dust continuum emission (Fedele et al. 2018). At the angular resolution of the early 1.3 mm data, the most prominent substructures are two bright rings at 75 AU and 130 AU, separated by two dust gaps at 62 AU and 103 AU.

Gas observations reveal that the disk morphology is not limited to the dust component. The C\(^{18}\)O emission follows a related but not identical radial structure, with a central peak, a deficit between the two main dust gaps, and an outer gas ring around 140 AU (Favre et al. 2019). Thermochemical modeling further suggests that the gas surface density is reduced in two broad radial intervals, approximately 54–70 AU and 87–119 AU, with a gas ring-like enhancement between them (Favre et al. 2019).

These results show that AS 209 contains coordinated substructures in both dust and gas, consistent with strong dynamical perturbations within the disk.

Dust in the disk

Dust in protoplanetary disks emits thermal radiation at millimeter wavelengths, making it possible to map its radial distribution with interferometric observations. In AS 209, the 1.3 mm continuum reveals that the dust is not distributed smoothly, but instead concentrated into rings separated by narrow gaps (Fedele et al. 2018).

The outer dust ring at 130 AU is especially important because it is spatially associated with an outer gas ring seen in C\(^{18}\)O, suggesting that the dust and gas structures are physically connected (Favre et al. 2019). In addition, the narrow widths of the gaps imply that the dust distribution is being shaped by localized dynamical processes rather than by a simple monotonic radial decline.

The continuum morphology of AS 209 is therefore consistent with a disk in which solids are being redistributed by pressure variations and gas–dust interactions, likely in connection with the gravitational influence of one or more forming planets.

Gas in the disk

The disk around AS 209 also contains a structured molecular gas component that can be studied through line emission from CO, \(^{13}\)CO, C\(^{18}\)O, and DCO\(^+\) (Favre et al. 2019). These tracers probe different observational regimes, but the C\(^{18}\)O line is especially important because it is optically thin, allowing the gas surface density perturbations to be studied more directly.

The gas disk extends beyond the region traced by the millimeter dust continuum, with CO emission detected at radii greater than 200 AU (Favre et al. 2019). The gas also shows a rotational pattern consistent with Keplerian motion, and the disk-averaged spectra are centered around a systemic velocity of 4.5 km s\(^{-1}\) (Favre et al. 2019).

A key result of the gas analysis is that the C\(^{18}\)O radial profile exhibits a deficit between the two main dust gaps, while DCO\(^+\) shows the opposite trend, becoming enhanced in the region between them (Favre et al. 2019). This indicates that the gas disk is dynamically and chemically structured, rather than smooth.

Evidence of planet formation

Several lines of evidence suggest that AS 209 is undergoing planet formation. The dust continuum exhibits narrow gaps and rings, while the gas observations show that these dust substructures are accompanied by real perturbations in the gas surface density (Fedele et al. 2018; Favre et al. 2019).

“Our findings confirm that the previously observed dust gaps are very likely due to perturbation of the gas surface density that is induced by a planet of at least 0.2 MJ in formation.”

Thermo-chemical modeling shows that the observed C\(^{18}\)O deficit between the two main dust gaps cannot be reproduced unless the gas surface density is reduced by a factor of roughly 5–10 within those regions (Favre et al. 2019). This means that the gaps are not merely features of the dust distribution, but correspond to actual perturbations in the gas disk.

Hydrodynamical simulations are consistent with this picture. In particular, models with a planet of 0.2–0.3 Jupiter masses located near 95–100 AU reproduce the depth and radial extent of the inferred gas perturbation (Favre et al. 2019). These simulations show that an embedded planet can open a gas gap, alter the dust-to-gas ratio, and create pressure structures that trap drifting dust grains in rings.

Taken together, the dust morphology, the gas surface density deficit, and the hydrodynamical modeling strongly support the interpretation that AS 209 hosts at least one forming giant planet in its outer disk.

Physical interpretation of AS 209

AS 209 is a particularly important disk because it links dust substructures, gas density perturbations, and planet–disk interaction models within the same system. The dust continuum shows clear rings and gaps, but the gas analysis demonstrates that these are not purely superficial dust features: the gas itself is depleted in the same broad radial zones (Favre et al. 2019).

This result is physically significant because planets are expected to perturb the surrounding gas through gravitational torques, opening gaps and redistributing material into adjacent rings. In AS 209, the observed combination of dust rings, gas depletion traced by C\(^{18}\)O, and DCO\(^+\) enhancement between the gaps is consistent with a disk in which a forming giant planet is actively modifying the local disk environment.

AS 209 therefore stands out as one of the best observational laboratories for studying how planet formation reshapes both the solid and gaseous components of a protoplanetary disk.
