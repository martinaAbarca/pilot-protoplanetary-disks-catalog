Location

PDS 70 is located at a distance of 113.43 ± 0.52 pc and belongs to the Upper Centaurus–Lupus subgroup of the Scorpius–Centaurus association (Keppler et al. 2018). Its central star is a young K7 T Tauri star, and the system age of 5.4 ± 1.0 Myr places it in an evolutionary stage where disk substructures and planet formation can still be observed directly (Keppler et al. 2018).

Overview of the system

PDS 70 is a young system composed of a pre-main-sequence T Tauri star surrounded by a transition disk. It is a particularly important object for planet formation studies because several key structures are observed in the same system:

  • a compact inner disk
  • a large cavity
  • a well-resolved outer dust ring
  • directly detected protoplanets inside the gap
  • evidence of active accretion onto those protoplanets

The inner disk survives close to the star and is detected both in scattered light and in ALMA continuum data, while the cavity separates this inner component from the bright outer ring (Keppler et al. 2018, 2019). Later observations also confirmed that PDS 70 b and PDS 70 c are still accreting, making the system one of the clearest known examples of planets forming inside a structured disk (Haffert et al. 2019).

Disk morphology

PDS 70 shows the typical morphology of a transition disk, but with a particularly rich substructure. The system contains a compact inner disk, a large cavity, and a bright outer ring. In polarized scattered light, the outer disk appears as an elliptical ring with non-uniform brightness. In ALMA continuum observations, the disk shows a bright outer dust ring near 74 au, a possible inner shoulder around 60 au, and a tentative spur/bridge feature that may connect the outer and inner regions (Keppler et al. 2018, 2019).

Because PDS 70 b and PDS 70 c are located inside the cavity, the morphology of the disk is directly linked to ongoing planet formation. This makes PDS 70 a particularly valuable system for studying planet–disk interaction and the clearing of large cavities by young planets (Keppler et al. 2018, 2019; Haffert et al. 2019).

Dust in the disk

Dust in PDS 70 is not distributed uniformly. Instead, it is organized into distinct substructures that depend on grain size. In scattered light, which mainly traces small grains in the disk surface, the system shows a large gap and an outer brightness peak near 54 au, together with emission from an inner dust disk (Keppler et al. 2018). In ALMA continuum observations, which trace larger grains, the radial profile shows a strong gap plus ring morphology, with the main ring peaking at 73.7 au and the flux inside the cavity reduced by more than 90% (Keppler et al. 2019).

This offset between the small-grain cavity wall and the large-grain millimeter ring is consistent with radial grain-size segregation. Small grains remain closer to the cavity wall, while larger grains accumulate farther out, likely in a region favored by the pressure structure of the disk (Keppler et al. 2018, 2019).

Gas in the disk

The gas component of PDS 70 was studied mainly through \(^{12}\)CO J=3−2 emission. Unlike the dust continuum, the gas traces an elevated emitting layer rather than the midplane, so its observed morphology is more extended and geometrically complex (Keppler et al. 2019). ALMA data show that the cavity is not completely empty in gas: there is an inner gas disk extending to about 15 au, a main CO depression centered near 23 au, and a possible structure connecting the inner disk and outer ring in the northwest region (Keppler et al. 2019).

The gas kinematics also show a deviation from Keplerian rotation inside about 0.8″ (~91 au). This is interpreted as evidence for a pressure gradient that helps explain why the large-grain dust ring lies much farther out than the orbit of PDS 70 b (Keppler et al. 2019).

Evidence of planet formation

Planet formation in PDS 70 is supported by three complementary lines of evidence. First, PDS 70 b was directly detected as a point source inside the cavity and confirmed at multiple epochs as a planetary-mass companion physically associated with the system (Keppler et al. 2018). Second, H\(\alpha\) emission was detected from both PDS 70 b and PDS 70 c, showing that both protoplanets are actively accreting gas (Haffert et al. 2019). Third, the disk itself shows clear signs of perturbation: the CO emission is depleted near the orbit of PDS 70 b and its rotation profile deviates from Keplerian motion, consistent with ongoing planet–disk interaction (Keppler et al. 2019).

Protoplanets

PDS 70 is one of the few systems where two protoplanets have been observed directly inside a large disk cavity. PDS 70 b was first detected in direct imaging, while PDS 70 c was later identified through H\(\alpha\) emission as a second accreting protoplanet (Keppler et al. 2018; Haffert et al. 2019).

Physical interpretation

PDS 70 is interpreted as a young planetary system still in formation, where the protoplanets are already reshaping the surrounding disk. The large cavity, the segregation between small and large dust grains, and the survival of an inner disk all support the idea that planets are sculpting the gap (Keppler et al. 2018). Later ALMA observations showed that the gas is depleted near the orbit of PDS 70 b and that the rotation profile is consistent with a pressure gradient capable of explaining why the main dust ring lies much farther out (Keppler et al. 2019).

That same ALMA study also suggested that a single planet may not be enough to explain the full observed morphology, which points to a more complex system architecture (Keppler et al. 2019). The later detection of PDS 70 c in H\(\alpha\) strongly supports that picture. Together, these results make PDS 70 a benchmark system for studying planet–disk interaction, gap opening, and accretion onto forming giant planets (Haffert et al. 2019).

Activity

In this activity, students will work with a FITS image of PDS 70 to explore how disk substructures can be identified through basic data-science tools. They will first load and visualize the image, then compute a radial brightness profile to identify the cavity and the outer ring. Finally, they will compare the measured locations of these features with the values reported in the literature and discuss why small grains in scattered light and large grains in millimeter continuum do not appear at exactly the same radius.
