Location

AS 209 is located in the Ophiuchus star-forming region, at a distance of 126 pc from the Sun (Fedele et al. 2018). Ophiuchus is a particularly valuable environment for the study of protoplanetary disks, as it hosts a rich population of young stellar systems observed across different evolutionary stages. Its significant number of deeply embedded sources, many with ages below about 1 Myr, makes this region especially well suited for investigating how circumstellar disks emerge and evolve during the earliest phases of star and planet formation.

General description of the system

HL Tau is a remarkably important young system because, although it is still described as a “Class I–II protostar” (ALMA Partnership et al. 2015), it already hosts a compact, massive disk together with a highly collimated outflow, reflecting an early yet highly active stage of evolution. ALMA observations revealed a clear sequence of “bright and dark rings” in the disk (ALMA Partnership et al. 2015), showing that a complex architecture is already in place at this initial stage. Moreover, the dark regions are “not devoid of emission” (ALMA Partnership et al. 2015), and their properties are consistent with dust growth and evolution, suggesting that they may be linked to the earliest stages of planet formation. This interpretation becomes clearer when one considers that ALMA primarily traces millimeter-sized dust: in the presence of embedded protoplanets, that dust can respond differently from the gas, making the gaps appear sharper in the dust component (“different response of the dust compared to the gas”; “it is easier to carve gaps in dust than in gas” (Dipierro et al. 2015)). Along the same lines, radiative transfer modeling indicates that the main gaps are “depleted by at least a factor of 10” in dust and that the outermost ring contains fewer millimeter-sized grains than the inner regions, pointing to more efficient grain growth closer to the star and/or radial migration (Pinte et al. 2016). In addition, the millimeter dust forms a “geometrically thin (scale height ≈1 AU at 100 AU)” layer (Pinte et al. 2016), which is interpreted as a clear sign of strong settling toward the midplane, precisely where solid material can become concentrated and continue to evolve.

Disk morphology

The morphology of the HL Tau disk is defined by a remarkably pronounced annular architecture, resolved by ALMA in unprecedented detail at 2.9, 1.3, and 0.87 mm. These observations reveal a pattern of “bright and dark rings observed at all wavelengths” (ALMA Partnership et al. 2015), demonstrating that these are genuine disk substructures rather than imaging artifacts. In the combined 1.0 mm image, the authors identify “seven pairs of ‘bright’ and ‘dark’ rings” (ALMA Partnership et al. 2015), organized as B1–B7 for the bright rings and D1–D7 for the dark gaps, revealing a complex and highly ordered radial distribution of material. Dipierro et al. (2015) further describe HL Tau as showing “concentric, axisymmetric gaps,” emphasizing the regular and symmetric character of this morphology. In their interpretation, this appearance can be understood because ALMA primarily traces millimeter-sized dust, and that component does not respond to embedded planets in the same way as the gas; as a result, while the gas may develop spiral structure, the dust tends to highlight rings and gaps because of the “different response of gas and dust” (Dipierro et al. 2015). In addition, the planets in their model are not presented as direct detections, but rather as a dynamical test of plausibility: the main observed morphology can be reproduced with “three embedded planets at 13.2, 32.3 and 68.8 au” (Dipierro et al. 2015), supporting the idea that the most prominent gaps may be linked to planet–disk interaction.

Dust in the disk

In HL Tau, dust is not distributed homogeneously, but instead exhibits a clearly differentiated radial and vertical structure. ALMA observations indicate a “pattern of bright and dark rings observed at all wavelengths,” and that the dark rings “are not devoid of emission” (ALMA Partnership et al. 2015), which indicates that the solid material does not disappear completely in those regions, but is instead redistributed within the disk. On the one hand, Pinte et al. show that the spectral index increases from the inner to the outer regions, indicating that the emission properties change with radius. In this context, the fact that the outermost ring is depleted in millimeter-sized dust is interpreted as evidence of “faster grain growth in the central regions and/or radial migration of the larger grains” (Pinte et al. 2016). Added to this is the fact that the main gaps show a drastic reduction in dust density relative to the adjacent rings, indicating that the solid material does not form a smooth disk, but is already strongly concentrated in some regions and depleted in others. Moreover, the millimeter dust is confined to a “geometrically thin (scale height ≈1 AU at 100 AU)” (Pinte et al. 2016) layer, which constitutes a clear sign of vertical settling toward the midplane. Taken together, this suggests that the millimeter continuum is dominated by large, settled grains, whereas reproducing the SED requires an additional population of submicron grains distributed in a much thicker structure, thus reinforcing the idea of strong dust stratification in the disk.

Gas in the disk

In HL Tau, the disk gas is not observed as a simple, isolated component, but rather as part of a system that remains embedded in an active environment. ALMA observations show that its study is affected by “spatial and kinematic confusion due to the extended ridge and outflow emission near the ambient velocity of vlsr = 6–7 km s−1” (ALMA Partnership et al. 2015), meaning that the disk signal is partially blended with that of the surrounding material. Even so, HCO+ made it possible to “spatially resolve the morphology of the HL Tau molecular gas disk for the first time” (ALMA Partnership et al. 2015), revealing a “roughly Keplerian” velocity distribution over a detectable range of 2.0 to 12.0 km s−1. Along the same lines, Pinte et al. show that both HCO+ and ^12CO display “a spatially compact component” and a “velocity gradient consistent with disk rotation” (Pinte et al. 2016), confirming that the brightest gas is truly associated with the disk and rotates around the star. Moreover, not all tracers describe this dynamics in the same way: CN and HCN “are only detected in absorption” (ALMA Partnership et al. 2015), and part of the observed asymmetry is interpreted as self-absorption linked to the outflow. Taken together, this picture shows that the gas in HL Tau follows an approximately Keplerian rotation, although its structure and kinematics are not entirely simple; indeed, while ALMA Partnership derives a stellar mass of about ~1.3 M☉, Pinte et al. conclude that the gas kinematics are more consistent with a star of ~1.7 M☉, reinforcing the view that this is a young, dynamic molecular disk whose detailed interpretation still requires further modeling.

Evidence of planet formation

Observationally, HL Tau provides evidence of planet formation: thanks to ALMA, we know that the disk material is already being dynamically sculpted. The disk gaps “are not devoid of emission” (ALMA Partnership et al. 2015), which means that the cavity is not empty, but instead corresponds to a region where the dust has been redistributed by a physical process consistent with planet–disk interaction. In addition, several rings show center offsets, and some substructures follow resonant proportions such as D1:D2:D3:D4 = 1:4:6:8; these dynamics—opening gaps, perturbing the orbits of disk material, and organizing structures within the disk—are evidence of a protoplanet altering the disk architecture. This interpretation is further supported by the simulations of Dipierro et al. (2015), in which “three embedded planets” were modeled at 13.2, 32.3, and 68.8 au, with masses of 0.2, 0.27, and 0.55 MJ, and it was concluded that this scenario can “reproduce most of the structures seen in the ALMA image”. In other words, the planets are not presented here as directly detected objects, but as a physically plausible explanation for why the disk displays such an ordered architecture: the protoplanets open gaps, push dust toward the edges of those gaps, and—because the dust responds more clearly than the gas—the final image observed by ALMA naturally takes on this morphology of bright and dark rings.

Physical interpretation

HL Tau can be physically interpreted as an extremely young system that remains in an active evolutionary stage, yet whose disk already exhibits a complex architecture that is unlikely to arise from a passive distribution of material alone. As ALMA observations describe it, HL Tau is “an excellent example of a system just emerging from its protostellar cocoon” (ALMA Partnership et al. 2015), and even at this stage it already shows bright rings, dark gaps, evidence of grain growth, and a radial organization suggesting that both dust and gas are being dynamically rearranged. In this context, Dipierro et al. provide a broader physical interpretation: the morphology of HL Tau can be understood as the outcome of interactions between dust, gas, and embedded planets, with the millimeter dust responding more visibly than the gas to gravitational perturbations and therefore producing the sharply defined rings and gaps seen by ALMA. Taken together, these studies converge on the same picture: HL Tau is not simply a young disk with substructures, but a system in which dust evolution, material redistribution, and the possible presence of protoplanets may already be actively sculpting the disk architecture.
