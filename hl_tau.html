Location

HL Tau is located in the Taurus Molecular Cloud, one of the closest star-forming regions to Earth. The system lies at a distance of approximately 140 parsecs (~450 light-years). Because of its proximity and young age, this region contains many protostars and protoplanetary disks that can be studied in detail.

Overview of the system

HL Tau is one of the most iconic protoplanetary disks ever observed. High-resolution observations obtained with the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) revealed a complex system of bright rings and dark gaps within the disk surrounding this very young star (ALMA Partnership et al. 2015).

These structures suggest that planet formation may begin much earlier than previously thought. With an estimated age of about 1–2 million years, HL Tau has become a key laboratory for studying the early stages of protoplanetary disk evolution and planet formation.

Physical models of the disk also indicate that millimeter-sized dust grains are concentrated in a very thin layer near the midplane and that turbulence in the system is relatively weak (Pinte et al. 2016). These conditions favor dust growth and the early formation of planetesimals.

Disk morphology

ALMA observations show that the disk of HL Tau contains a complex radial structure composed of bright rings and dark gaps (ALMA Partnership et al. 2015). In total, 14 substructures have been identified, organized into seven bright rings (B1–B7) and seven dark gaps (D1–D7).

These features appear consistently across multiple millimeter wavelengths, confirming that they are real physical structures rather than observational artifacts.

The rings correspond to regions with a high concentration of dust, while the gaps indicate areas where the density of material is significantly reduced. This pattern suggests that strong dynamical processes are shaping the disk, possibly related to interactions between the disk and forming planets.

Dust in the disk

Dust in protoplanetary disks emits thermal radiation at millimeter wavelengths, allowing astronomers to study its distribution using interferometric observations. The spectral index of the emission provides information about the size and properties of the dust grains (ALMA Partnership et al. 2015).

In HL Tau, the central regions and some bright rings appear optically thick, indicating a high concentration of solid material. In contrast, the dark gaps exhibit lower brightness and higher spectral index values, suggesting that these regions contain less dust.

The dust opacity index β ≈ 0.3–0.8 indicates that dust grains have grown significantly beyond the typical size of interstellar grains, an important step toward the formation of planetesimals (ALMA Partnership et al. 2015).

Disk models also show that millimeter-sized grains are strongly concentrated near the midplane, forming a geometrically thin layer. At a distance of about 100 AU, the vertical thickness of this dust layer is only ≈2 AU, indicating strong dust settling (Pinte et al. 2016).

This behavior implies that turbulence in the disk is relatively weak, with an estimated turbulent viscosity of α ≈ 3 × 10⁻⁴ (Pinte et al. 2016). Hydrodynamic simulations further show that small grains remain coupled to the gas, while larger millimeter grains migrate toward pressure maxima at the edges of gaps, where they accumulate into rings (Dipierro et al. 2015).

Gas in the disk

The disk around HL Tau also contains a large amount of molecular gas, observable through spectral lines of molecules such as CO, HCO⁺, CN, and HCN (ALMA Partnership et al. 2015). These emission lines allow astronomers to measure the velocity of the gas through the Doppler effect.

Observations show that the gas follows a Keplerian rotation pattern around the central star, with velocities ranging from approximately 2 to 12 km/s. Position–velocity diagrams of CO emission indicate that the mass of the central star is close to 1.7 solar masses (ALMA Partnership et al. 2015).

The gas disk also extends farther than the region where millimeter dust emission is detected, which is typical in protoplanetary disks.

Evidence of planet formation

Several lines of evidence suggest that HL Tau may already be forming planets. The dark gaps observed in the disk correspond to regions where the dust density is significantly lower than in neighboring rings (ALMA Partnership et al. 2015).

Models indicate that the formation of the largest gaps may require the removal of up to ≈40 Earth masses of dust, material that could have been incorporated into growing planetary cores (Dipierro et al. 2015).

Hydrodynamic simulations show that embedded planets can generate these structures through gravitational perturbations. Models including three protoplanets located at approximately 13.2 AU, 32.3 AU, and 68.8 AU reproduce many of the observed features of the disk. The estimated masses of these planets are about 0.2, 0.27, and 0.55 Jupiter masses (Dipierro et al. 2015).

These simulations also show that gaps are easier to open in dust than in gas, meaning that the structures observed in millimeter continuum images primarily trace the distribution of solid material.

Physical interpretation of HL Tau

Taken together, high-resolution ALMA observations and physical models indicate that HL Tau is an extremely young system in which planet formation may already be underway. The presence of massive dust rings, deep gaps, and millimeter-sized grains suggests that solid material in the disk is evolving toward the formation of planetesimals. Hydrodynamic simulations indicate that several young protoplanets with masses comparable to giant planets could explain the observed disk structures (Dipierro et al. 2015).

Activity

La actividad consiste en analizar una imagen del disco protoplanetario HL Tau obtenida con el telescopio ALMA para identificar las subestructuras presentes en el disco. Los estudiantes deberán cargar una imagen bidimensional del continuo de polvo (por ejemplo, en formato .fits o como matriz de intensidades) y aplicar herramientas básicas de ciencia de datos para calcular el perfil radial de brillo, es decir, cómo varía la intensidad de la emisión con la distancia al centro del disco. A partir de este perfil podrán identificar máximos y mínimos de intensidad que corresponden a anillos brillantes y brechas (gaps) en la distribución del polvo. El objetivo de la actividad es mostrar cómo el análisis computacional de imágenes astronómicas permite detectar subestructuras en discos protoplanetarios y comprender mejor los procesos físicos asociados a la formación de planetas.
