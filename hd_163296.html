Location

HD 163296 is a young Herbig Ae star located at a distance of approximately 101–122 parsecs (~330–400 light-years) from Earth (Isella et al. 2016; Rab et al. 2020).

The star belongs to the class of intermediate-mass pre–main sequence stars and is surrounded by one of the most extensively studied protoplanetary disks. Because the disk is large, bright, and relatively isolated, it provides an ideal laboratory for investigating the processes that shape planet-forming disks.

Overview of the system

HD 163296 is one of the best-studied protoplanetary disks with resolved substructures. High-resolution ALMA observations revealed a system of rings and gaps in the millimeter dust continuum emission at radii of roughly 60 AU, 100 AU, and 160 AU (Isella et al. 2016).

Subsequent studies showed that the gas disk exhibits a more complex structure. Thermo-chemical modeling demonstrated that some radial features observed in CO emission are strongly influenced by temperature gradients and dust opacity effects rather than solely by changes in gas density (Rab et al. 2020).

In addition, high-resolution spectroscopic observations have revealed localized deviations from Keplerian rotation, which are interpreted as evidence for embedded planets perturbing the gas velocity field within the disk (pinte2020?).

Together, these results indicate that HD 163296 is a key system for studying the interaction between forming planets and their natal disks.

Disk morphology

ALMA observations show that the disk of HD 163296 contains a radial sequence of bright rings and dark gaps in the dust continuum emission (Isella et al. 2016).

The three most prominent dust gaps are located at approximately:

  • ~60 AU
  • ~100 AU
  • ~160 AU

These substructures likely correspond to regions where the surface density of solid material has been reduced.

The dust rings surrounding the gaps are interpreted as pressure maxima, where millimeter-sized particles accumulate as a consequence of radial drift and particle trapping within the disk.

Dust in the disk

Dust in protoplanetary disks emits thermal radiation at millimeter wavelengths. ALMA observations at 1.3 mm revealed that the dust distribution in HD 163296 contains several radial depletions associated with the gaps (Isella et al. 2016).

Modeling of the continuum emission indicates that the dust surface density drops dramatically inside the gaps, with depletion factors reaching:

  • >100 in the inner gap
  • ~70 in the middle gap
  • ~6 in the outer gap

These results suggest that solid particles are strongly concentrated in the rings, where pressure maxima trap drifting dust grains.

Dust rings therefore represent locations where conditions may favor the formation of planetesimals, the building blocks of planets.

Gas in the disk

The gas disk in HD 163296 has been observed through molecular emission lines such as ¹²CO, ¹³CO, and C¹⁸O (Isella et al. 2016; Rab et al. 2020).

Unlike the dust, the gas emission is more extended and does not display equally sharp ringed structures. The gas disk extends out to approximately 550 AU.

Thermo-chemical modeling has shown that some radial intensity features observed in ¹²CO J = 2–1 are caused by radiative transfer effects rather than true gas density variations. In particular, emission from the back side of the disk can be partially absorbed by optically thick dust rings, producing apparent peaks in the radial intensity profile (Rab et al. 2020).

This demonstrates that interpreting gas observations requires detailed modeling of both the dust distribution and the disk temperature structure.

Evidence of planet formation

Several lines of evidence suggest that HD 163296 hosts forming planets.

Hydrodynamic simulations indicate that the gaps in the dust disk can be reproduced by embedded planets with masses comparable to those of Saturn or Jupiter (Isella et al. 2016).

In addition, measurements of the gas velocity field reveal localized deviations from Keplerian rotation, which are interpreted as perturbations produced by planets embedded within the disk (pinte2020?).

The inferred properties of these candidate planets are approximately:

Radius (AU) Estimated mass
~100 AU ~1 MJ
~165 AU ~1.3 MJ

These measurements represent one of the first cases in which forming planets have been inferred from direct perturbations in the gas velocity field of a protoplanetary disk.

Physical interpretation of HD 163296

The structure of the HD 163296 disk is likely shaped by a combination of physical processes.

The presence of deep dust gaps and pressure maxima suggests that planet–disk interaction plays a major role in sculpting the disk morphology.

However, thermo-chemical modeling shows that the interpretation of gas emission lines is complicated by temperature gradients and radiative transfer effects within the disk (Rab et al. 2020).

Together, the observations of dust rings, gas dynamics, and velocity perturbations indicate that HD 163296 is a system in which planet formation is actively reshaping the disk structure.

Activity

En esta actividad los estudiantes analizarán una imagen del continuo de polvo del disco protoplanetario HD 163296 obtenida con el telescopio ALMA utilizando herramientas de programación en Python. La imagen del disco se cargará en el entorno de trabajo como una matriz bidimensional de intensidades, donde cada píxel representa la cantidad de emisión de polvo detectada en una región específica del disco. El primer paso consiste en cargar la imagen del disco en el programa y visualizarla para identificar la posición aproximada del centro del sistema, que corresponde a la ubicación de la estrella. A partir de este punto central, los estudiantes calcularán la distancia radial de cada píxel, es decir, la distancia de cada punto de la imagen con respecto al centro del disco. Este cálculo permite transformar la información espacial de la imagen en una medida de radio. Una vez calculadas las distancias, los estudiantes agruparán los píxeles que se encuentran a radios similares y calcularán la intensidad promedio en cada anillo radial. Este procedimiento permite construir el perfil radial de brillo del disco, que describe cómo cambia la intensidad de la emisión del polvo a medida que aumenta la distancia desde la estrella. Posteriormente, los estudiantes graficarán la intensidad promedio en función del radio. En este gráfico se observarán zonas donde la intensidad aumenta, correspondientes a anillos de polvo, y zonas donde la intensidad disminuye, correspondientes a brechas o gaps en la distribución del material del disco. Al analizar este perfil radial será posible identificar tres mínimos principales ubicados aproximadamente en 60 AU, 100 AU y 160 AU, que coinciden con las brechas observadas en estudios de alta resolución del disco HD 163296. Finalmente, los estudiantes interpretarán estos resultados desde el punto de vista físico. Las brechas observadas indican regiones donde la cantidad de polvo es menor, lo que sugiere que el material del disco ha sido redistribuido. En sistemas como HD 163296, estas estructuras se interpretan como posibles evidencias de interacción entre el disco y planetas en formación, que pueden abrir brechas en el material del disco mientras orbitan la estrella. Esta actividad permite mostrar cómo el análisis computacional de imágenes astronómicas puede revelar la estructura interna de los discos protoplanetarios y proporcionar pistas sobre los procesos de formación planetaria.
