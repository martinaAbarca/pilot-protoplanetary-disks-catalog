Location

HD 163296 is a young Herbig Ae star located at a distance of approximately 101–122 parsecs (~330–400 light-years) from Earth (Isella et al. 2016; Rab et al. 2020).

The star belongs to the class of intermediate-mass pre–main sequence stars and is surrounded by one of the most extensively studied protoplanetary disks. Because the disk is large, bright, and relatively isolated, it provides an ideal laboratory for investigating the processes that shape planet-forming disks.

General description of the system

HD 163296 is a protoplanetary disk surrounding a Herbig Ae star whose dust morphology has made it a benchmark system for the study of radial substructures; indeed, it has been described as “the third system known to host multiple rings in dust emission at millimetre wavelength” (Diep et al., 2019). On global scales, the system combines a large, dynamically ordered gas disk—“The gas disc is in Keplerian motion and has a radius of about 550 au, and the millimetre continuum emission from solid particles is confined to within 250 au of the star”—with a more compact solid component, where earlier observations resolved “three concentric gap-ring pairs,” with gaps at ~54, ~100, and 160 au (Diep et al., 2019). Higher-resolution observations further show that this architecture is not limited to simple concentric rings: “The continuum observations resolve and allow us to characterize the previously discovered dust rings at radii of 68 and 100. They also reveal new small-scale structures, such as a dark gap at 10 au, a bright ring at 15 au, a dust crescent at a radius of 55 au, and several fainter azimuthal asymmetries” (Isella et al., 2018). In this sense, the dust distribution is not uniform but exhibits both radial and azimuthal variations, since “The dust continuum emission is characterized by several asymmetric structures,” and these features “corroborate the hypothesis that the complex structure of the HD 163296 disk is the result of the gravitational interaction with yet-unseen planets” (Isella et al., 2018).

Disk morphology

The morphology of HD 163296 can be described as that of a dust disk with a hierarchical architecture of rings and gaps: in the 1.3 mm continuum, Diep et al. (2019) note the presence of “three concentric gap-ring pairs,” and that at higher resolution the disk shows “much deeper gaps” between the rings, more clearly defining the radial redistribution of dust. To this global structure, Isella et al. (2018) add “three new morphological features,” including an inner dark gap at ~10 au, a bright ring at ~15 au, and a central azimuthal asymmetry, while in the outer disk “the outermost features B67, D86, B100, D145, and B155, are well described by concentric circles.” Beyond this concentric organization, the disk is not perfectly uniform: “the image of the residuals shows fainter asymmetries along the B67 and B100 dust rings” (Isella et al., 2018), and Diep et al. (2019) further conclude that the outer gap and outer ring display a “significant modulation of their morphology and intensity.” Altogether, HD 163296 exhibits a morphology in which gaps, rings, and local asymmetries coexist within a globally thin and well-organized disk structure.

Dust in the disk

In the case of HD 163296, dust dynamics—that is, the radial migration of solids toward local gas-pressure maxima—is interpreted as a key mechanism in the formation of the rings. These bright rings—“the rings B67 and B100 of the HD 163296 disk, have a radial width that is narrower than the estimated pressure scale height of the gaseous disk” (Isella et al. 2018)—suggest that the dust is concentrated and confined within relatively narrow regions. In addition to ring and gap structures, the disk also shows asymmetric dust concentrations: “new small-scale structures, such as a dark gap at 10 au, a bright ring at 15 au, a dust crescent at a radius of 55 au, and several fainter azimuthal asymmetries” (Isella et al. 2018). Among these asymmetries, the most prominent correspond to crescents, since “The dust continuum emission is characterized by several asymmetric structures. The most prominent consist in crescents centered at about 4 and 55 au” (Isella et al. 2018). For this reason, the dusty environment of the disk should be understood as dynamic rather than perfectly axisymmetric. This interpretation is further reinforced by the kinematic evidence presented by Pinte et al. (2020), who note that “upon deprojection, we find that all of the candidate planets lie within a gap and/or at the end of a spiral detected in dust continuum emission.” In other words, the dust substructures not only describe where solid material is concentrated or redistributed, but also spatially coincide with possible embedded perturbers, supporting the idea that part of the observed dust architecture is being sculpted by forming protoplanets.

Gas in the disk

According to the gas structure of HD 163296, the gaseous disk does not end where the millimeter dust continuum is observed, but instead extends much farther outward: “The CO emission extends far beyond the outer edge of millimeter-wave continuum emission up to an angular distance of 5 6, or about 560 au, from the central star” (Isella et al. 2018). To understand how its geometry is observed, it is important to note that the CO-traced gas does not appear as a single layer, but as two warm emitting surfaces on the front and rear sides of the disk: “The CO emission observed at intermediate velocities … shows a characteristic ‘dragonfly’ structure tracing both the front and rear warm CO-emitting layers of the disk” (Isella et al. 2018). The rear-side emission appears fainter because “The emission coming from the rear of the disk is fainter than that from the front due to a combination of lower temperature of the emitting gas and absorption from the intervening material” (Isella et al. 2018). The separation between these two surfaces therefore makes it possible to infer the vertical structure of the gas, since “the separation between the front and rear CO emission measures the vertical geometry of the CO-emitting surfaces” (Isella et al. 2018). In addition, the CO temperature decreases with distance from the star, reaching values “as high as 120 K in the innermost disk regions and as low as 20 K at the disk outer edge” (Isella et al. 2018). Finally, the global motion of the gas follows an ordered rotation dominated by stellar gravity: “The overall kinematics of the CO gas is well described by Keplerian rotation around a star with a mass of about 2.0 M⊙” (Isella et al. 2018). Unlike the dust, the gas does not trace prominent rings and gaps, but instead appears in a smoother way, since “The maps of the 12CO, 13CO, and C18O J = 2 − 1 emission do not show such structures but reveal a change in the slope of the radial intensity profile across the positions of the dark rings in the continuum image” (Isella et al. 2016). This shows that what matters is not only how much gas is present, but also how it is redistributed relative to the dust, because “we deduce that the gas-to-dust ratio varies across the disk and, in particular, it increases by at least a factor 5 within the inner dust gap compared to adjacent regions of the disk” (Isella et al. 2016), suggesting that in this inner region the dust is more depleted than the gas.

Evidence of planet formation

The architecture of HD 163296, particularly its gaps and rings, is linked to real perturbations in the disk gas rather than to changes in dust opacity alone, as shown by the fact that “The recent detection of deviations from Keplerian rotation at the location of the gaps and rings by Teague et al. (2018) confirms that these structures correspond to variations in the gas pressure (i.e., variations in the gas density and/or temperature), as opposed to radial changes in the dust opacity” (Isella et al. 2018). In this context, comparisons between the observations and planet–disk interaction models suggest that several of these gaps may have been carved by planets, since “The comparison between the observations and planet–disk interaction models indicates that the density gaps might have been carved by planets with masses between 0.5 and 1 MJ orbiting at 48, 86, and 131 au from the central star” (Isella et al. 2018). The paper also shows that this evidence does not rely on a single scenario, because under very low-viscosity conditions “the observed multiple ring structure may also result from the gravitational interaction with a single planet that is less massive than Saturn at 100 au orbital radius” (Isella et al. 2018). In addition, substructures such as the crescent observed inside D45 reinforce this interpretation, since “features similar to the crescent observed inside the D45 gap (inset b of Figure 1) are naturally produced by the planet–disk interaction whenever the disk viscosity is low (α < 10−3) and the planet mass is above about 0.1 MJ” (Isella et al. 2018). Finally, the continuum asymmetries are not interpreted merely as morphological details, but as further support for this hypothesis, because “these asymmetries trace local variations in the dust densities and support the hypothesis that the HD 163296 disk is shaped by the gravitational interaction with yet-unseen planets” (Isella et al. 2018). This interpretation is extended by recent dynamical studies, in which HD 163296 “has been proposed to host at least four giant planets on wide orbits as well as a massive planetesimal disc” (Polychroni et al. 2025), so that the evidence for planet formation is not limited to the observed morphology, but also includes its consequences for the solid material in the system. Within that framework, “in our simulations the formation of giant planets in the HD 163296 system creates a large population of dynamically excited planetesimals, the majority of which originate from beyond the CO snowline” (Polychroni et al. 2025), and “HD 163296’s giant planets between 50 and 260 au are capable of delivering large amounts of planetesimals inwards of 40 au” (Polychroni et al. 2025), suggesting that the presence of young giant planets may not only sculpt rings and gaps, but also redistribute material toward the inner regions of the system.

Physical interpretation of HD 163296

HD 163296 can be physically interpreted as a disk in which dust is not distributed uniformly, but instead tends to accumulate in specific regions of the gas. In particular, “Dullemond et al. (2018) finds that many of the continuum rings revealed by DSHARP observations, including the rings B67 and B100 of the HD 163296 disk, have a radial width that is narrower than the estimated pressure scale height of the gaseous disk, and conclude that this is strong evidence that these rings have formed by radial migration of dust particles toward local maxima of the gas pressure” (Isella et al. 2018). This suggests that the rings are not simply patterns seen in the image, but regions where dust has migrated and accumulated because the local gas pressure favors it. At the same time, this architecture develops within a thin disk, and the outer region is not perfectly uniform, since “The disc is shown to be thin … Significant modulation of the intensity of the outer ring emission is revealed and discussed” (Diep et al. 2019). This complexity cannot be explained by smooth dust trapping alone, because “the observed asymmetries in the dust continuum emission corroborate the hypothesis that the complex structure of the HD 163296 disk is the result of the gravitational interaction with yet-unseen planets” (Isella et al. 2018). Meanwhile, the gas preserves an overall ordered motion, given that “The three line emissions display remarkably similar kinematics dominated by Keplerian rotation. An upper limit of 9% of the rotation velocity has been placed on a possible in-fall velocity” (Diep et al. 2019). Taken together, these results suggest a system in which the rings arise from dust concentration at gas-pressure maxima, while the more complex structures, such as asymmetries and brightness modulations, point to active interaction between the disk and possible forming planets.
